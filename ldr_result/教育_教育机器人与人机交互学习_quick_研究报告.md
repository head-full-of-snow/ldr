The intersection of robotics and human-computer interaction (HCI) in the context of medical and industrial applications has undergone a significant paradigm shift in 2025-2026, moving from simple teleoperation to "embodied intelligence" and collaborative autonomy. Recent literature highlights several critical advancements in educational machine learning, ethical frameworks, and technical integration.

**1. Shift from Disembodied to Embodied Intelligence in Interaction**
A major conceptual advancement is the transition from traditional AI to "embodied intelligence" in clinical settings. In fields like stomatology, previous AI models suffered from a "last centimeter" bottleneck due to weak environmental perception and a lack of dynamic interaction [6]. The new paradigm emphasizes a closed loop of "perception-decision-action," allowing robots to interact more naturally with their environment [6]. This aligns with broader trends in cognitive robotics, which aim to "free" robots from pre-programmed assembly lines, enabling them to learn from experience and adapt to real-world situations rather than relying solely on static algorithms [49]. This shift is crucial for HCI, as it allows robots to perceive human intent and environmental changes dynamically, rather than just executing rigid commands.

**2. Machine Learning in Surgical Education and Training**
Machine learning (ML) is transforming how surgical outcomes are predicted and how surgeons are trained. In robotic-assisted total knee arthroplasty (RA-TKA), ML models are being integrated to predict patient-specific outcomes, offering advantages in surgical planning and personalized care [1]. This data-driven approach allows for a feedback loop where the robot learns from historical data to optimize future interactions and procedures. Furthermore, the integration of AI into surgical training requires a new pedagogical model. Educators are developing "augmented caring pedagogy" to teach nursing and midwifery students how to integrate AI and robotics into person-centered care, highlighting the need for educators to reassess their readiness and teaching methods [43]. This suggests that HCI in education is not just about technical skills but also about understanding the relational aspects of care when mediated by machines.

**3. Ethical and Relational Dimensions of Human-Robot Interaction**
The introduction of AI-driven robots into healthcare has raised profound ethical questions regarding the nature of care and human interaction. Critics argue that while AI can facilitate work, it lacks the empathy and compassion required for "humanized care," potentially undermining the relational aspects of healthcare [4]. In caregiving contexts, such as dementia care, robots are used to relieve staff burdens, but this raises concerns about "relational autonomy" and whether machines can truly provide care, which involves evaluative and relational aspects beyond technical compliance [7], [21].

Moreover, healthcare professionals, particularly nurses, express concern that AI might undermine holistic, intuitive, and experience-based clinical judgment, fearing job replacement and the erosion of safe patient care foundations [8]. This highlights a critical HCI challenge: ensuring that human-robot interaction preserves the human element of care rather than reducing it to efficiency metrics. The distinction between robots and AI is also emphasized, noting that they are not a single moral category and require distinct ethical considerations for conscious and non-conscious systems [32].

**4. Legal, Regulatory, and Safety Frameworks**
The integration of AI and robotics in surgery introduces significant medicolegal and ethical challenges, particularly regarding liability and responsibility [5], [16]. For instance, in pediatric surgery, the unique legal status of children presents distinct ethical and legal concerns that require specific strategies for clinicians and policymakers [40]. The deployment of AI-native 6G healthcare systems, which enable real-time remote robotic surgery, requires strict alignment with regulatory frameworks like the EU AI Act and GDPR, yet current studies show limited integration of legal requirements into system design [10].

Safety and precision remain paramount. In complex environments, such as unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) with robotic arms, AI is used to compensate for dynamic disturbances like wind and aerodynamic turbulence, ensuring precise control and safety [11]. Similarly, in oral and maxillofacial surgery, while AI enhances planning and robotic support, ethical and legal governance remains unsettled, requiring careful scoping to map risk domains [33].

**5. Emerging Technologies and Future Directions**
Future developments include the use of digital twins for surgery, where a dynamic, patient-specific virtual model mirrors the physical patient in real-time, integrating data from robotic manipulators and biosensors to enhance planning and guidance [47]. This represents a sophisticated form of HCI, allowing for continuous feedback and adjustment during procedures. Additionally, the convergence of AI, robotics, and 6G networks is reshaping healthcare through real-time connectivity, enabling remote telesurgery and emergency telemedicine, though this requires overcoming significant technical and ethical hurdles [10], [39].

In summary, the latest research indicates that educational machine learning and HCI in robotics are evolving towards more embodied, adaptive, and ethically aware systems. While technical capabilities in perception, decision-making, and action are improving [6], [49], the focus is increasingly on integrating these technologies within robust ethical, legal, and educational frameworks to ensure they enhance rather than diminish human-centric care and safety [4], [5], [43].
