Recent scientific advancements in intelligent scheduling and production optimization are characterized by the convergence of artificial intelligence (AI), the Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT), and distributed computing architectures. These developments aim to address challenges related to latency, data privacy, interpretability, and human-AI collaboration in smart manufacturing environments. The following analysis synthesizes key trends from recent literature (2025–2026).

### 1. Cloud-Edge-End Collaborative Architectures and Resource Scheduling
A major trend in production optimization is the shift from centralized cloud computing to distributed cloud-edge-end architectures. This shift is driven by the need for low-latency processing and data privacy in smart factories [1]. Traditional cloud-based digital twins often suffer from high latency and limited scalability, prompting the integration of edge AI and federated learning to enable real-time physics-based co-simulation [10].

Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has emerged as a critical tool for optimizing resource scheduling in these distributed environments. Studies indicate that DRL effectively addresses issues such as increased latency and task dropouts caused by dynamic changes in edge node loads [2]. For instance, research on cloud-edge-end collaborative computing demonstrates that DRL can optimize collaborative resource management in complex systems, such as distribution networks with high-frequency data acquisition [19]. Furthermore, novel frameworks like Information Bottleneck-Enhanced Reinforcement Learning (IBE) have been proposed to solve operation research problems by integrating information-theoretic regularization, thereby improving stability and exploration efficiency in high-dimensional state spaces [11].

### 2. Digital Twins and Generative AI Integration
Digital Twins (DTs) are evolving from static simulation models into dynamic, AI-driven systems that enable autonomous decision-making. The integration of Generative AI (GAI) and Predictive AI (PAI) into Digital Twin systems allows for augmented training data, geometric inspection, and intelligent process optimization [9]. These AI-enabled Digital Twins bridge the physical and virtual domains, enhancing the ability to acquire process knowledge and control system variables autonomously [8].

In specific industrial applications, such as rail transit construction, integrated cloud-network digital intelligence platforms utilize 3D digital twins for real-time prediction and AI-driven scheduling, creating a closed-loop control system that improves operational efficiency [20]. Similarly, in offshore wind farm maintenance, UAV inspection path optimization uses hybrid algorithms (e.g., OPTION-A*-DQN) to address path redundancy and mission omissions, demonstrating the practical application of intelligent scheduling in logistics and maintenance [12].

### 3. Explainable AI (XAI) and Human-AI Collaboration
Despite the high accuracy of data-driven models, their "black-box" nature poses a barrier to industrial trust and regulatory compliance [6]. Consequently, there is a significant research focus on Explainable AI (XAI) to enhance transparency in critical manufacturing applications [7]. Hybrid modeling approaches that combine physics-based interpretability with data-driven accuracy are being developed to ensure both reliability and performance [6].

The impact of explainability extends to human factors in production management. Research indicates that trust calibration is critical for productive human-AI collaboration, particularly in job shop scheduling. Providing brief, effective explanations of AI decisions can significantly improve operator trust and reliance, thereby facilitating smoother adoption of intelligent systems [4]. This aligns with the Industry 5.0 paradigm, which emphasizes human centrality and resilience, requiring that intelligent systems be transparent and adaptable to human workflows [13].

### 4. Intelligent Quality Control and Predictive Maintenance
AI is transforming quality control and maintenance from reactive to predictive and preventive measures. In predictive maintenance, the convergence of AI and IIoT (AIoT) addresses the limitations of traditional methods in complex, data-driven environments, enabling adaptive operations and intelligent automation [17]. For example, in metal cutting processes, multi-sensor datasets integrating sound, vibration, and controller data are used for anomaly detection, allowing for real-time identification of tool defects and process abnormalities [15].

Vision-based AI is also being applied to complex manufacturing processes. In adaptive peen forming for aerospace panels, vision-based AI enables closed-loop smart manufacturing by providing spatially resolved deformation assessments, overcoming the limitations of indirect proxies like Almen intensity [14]. Additionally, AI-driven non-destructive testing is being utilized for quality control in emerging materials, such as carbon-negative biopolymer-bound soil composites, ensuring structural integrity before hardening occurs [16].

### 5. Cross-Industry Applications and Data Infrastructure
The principles of intelligent scheduling and optimization are being extended beyond traditional manufacturing. In pharmaceutical production, AI-powered information management frameworks are enhancing Quality-by-Design (QbD) principles, consolidating structured data to improve predictive quality control and regulatory compliance [18]. Similarly, in the food sector, machine learning is optimizing precision feeding and packaging life cycles, reducing costs and waste through data-driven insights [22], [23].

However, the implementation of these systems faces challenges related to legacy infrastructure and data silos [3]. The development of open-access datasets, such as the Multi-Sensor and MTConnect (MSM) dataset for CNC milling, is crucial for training robust AI models for anomaly detection in industrial settings [15].

### Conclusion
The latest research demonstrates that intelligent scheduling and production optimization are no longer solely about algorithmic efficiency but involve a holistic integration of distributed computing (edge/cloud), explainable AI for human trust, and digital twins for real-time simulation. Future developments will likely focus on further refining hybrid AI models, enhancing human-centric interfaces in Industry 5.0, and addressing data privacy and scalability in large-scale IoT deployments [1], [13], [17].
