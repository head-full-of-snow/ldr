Based on the provided new source and previous knowledge, the latest research progress in human-machine collaboration and intelligent assembly highlights a significant shift towards leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) to reduce cognitive load in complex teleoperation tasks.

A key development in this field is the introduction of frameworks designed for single-operator bimanual teleoperation. Traditionally, controlling two robotic arms simultaneously imposes high cognitive and coordination demands on a single human operator. While assigning separate operators to each arm can distribute the workload, it often introduces ambiguities in decision authority and can degrade overall operational efficiency [1]. To address these challenges, recent research proposes the Bimanual Teleoperation Large Language Model Assistant (BTLA) framework. This system functions as an intelligent co-pilot, utilizing LLMs to augment a single operator's motor control capabilities, thereby facilitating more efficient natural language interaction and control [1]. This approach represents a critical advancement in intelligent assembly and teleoperation by integrating high-level semantic understanding with low-level motor control, allowing for more intuitive and less burdensome human-machine collaboration.

According to [1], this framework aims to overcome the limitations of traditional multi-operator setups by providing a unified control interface that enhances efficiency and reduces the complexity of decision-making for the human operator. The integration of LLMs into teleoperation systems signifies a move towards more adaptive and cognitively supportive human-machine teams in industrial and assembly contexts.
